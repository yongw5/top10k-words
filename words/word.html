    actions speak louder than words
     

    (saying) what a person actually does means more than what they say they will do
    bandy words (with somebody)
     

    (old-fashioned) to argue with somebody or speak rudely to them
    be a dirty word
     

    to be a subject or an idea that people think is bad or immoral Profit is not a dirty word around here.
    be lost for words
     

    to be so surprised, confused, etc. that you do not know what to say
    by word of mouth
     

    because people tell each other and not because they read about it The news spread by word of mouth.
    eat your words
     

    to admit that what you said was wrong eat your wordsbreak
    famous last words
     

    (saying) people sometimes say Famous last words!when they think somebody is being too confident about something that is going to happen ‘Everything's under control.’ ‘Famous last words!’ This phrase refers to a collection of quotations of the dying words of famous people.

    (not) get a word in edgeways (British English) (North American English (not) get a word in edgewise)
     

    (not) to be able to say anything because somebody else is speaking too much When Mary starts talking, no one else can get a word in edgeways.
    hang on somebody’s words/on somebody’s every word
     

    to listen with great attention to somebody you admire
    have a word in somebody’s ear
     

    (British English) to speak to somebody privately about something
    have/exchange words (with somebody) (about something)
     

    (especially British English) to have an argument with somebody We've had words. Words were exchanged.

    not have a good word to say for somebody/something

    not mince (your) words
     

    to say something in a direct way even though it might offend other people They were severely criticized by the chairman, who was not a man to mince his words.

    weigh your words

    (upon) my word

    word for word

    somebody’s word is their bond
